# Topic: CHILDBIRTH AND NEWBORN CARE (LABOR TO 28 DAYS POSTPARTUM)

[CONTEXTUAL GUARDRAIL LOCK]: This document details the processes from the onset of labor through the first 28 days of a newborn's life (neonatal period). It covers birth preparedness, immediate actions after delivery like delayed cord clamping and skin-to-skin contact, initiation of breastfeeding, routine newborn care, identification of danger signs, and essential postpartum care for the mother. It also includes relevant government financial assistance schemes for delivery and maternity.

## 2. Executive Guidance & Context Layer
<executive_brief_block>
### A. Long Comprehensive Summary
[Target:: ChildbirthAndNewbornCare | Milestone:: Overview | Action:: Summary] This document provides a comprehensive guide to the period spanning from the onset of labor to 28 days postpartum. It begins with the crucial steps of birth preparedness, which include creating a birth plan, selecting a supportive companion, preparing an essential delivery bag, and arranging for finances and emergency transport. The guide emphasizes the importance of immediate medical consultation at the start of labor. Upon birth, a series of critical actions are detailed for newborn well-being, collectively known as essential newborn care. These include wiping the baby dry, delaying the first bath for at least 24 hours, and ensuring warmth. A central component is Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC), which involves early and prolonged skin-to-skin contact and is proven to stabilize temperature, promote breastfeeding, and prevent infections, especially in low birth weight (LBW) infants. The document strongly advocates for initiating breastfeeding within the first hour of life to provide the baby with colostrum, the nutrient-rich first milk that acts as a first immunization. Routine care for the newborn during the first 28 days includes keeping the umbilical cord stump clean and dry without applying any substances, monitoring for jaundice, and ensuring safe sleep practices, such as placing the baby on their back. A critical section is dedicated to identifying newborn danger signs, such as poor feeding, high temperature, or fast breathing, and outlines the necessity of seeking immediate care. The guide also covers the at-birth immunization schedule, including BCG, OPV (zero dose), and Hepatitis B vaccines. For the mother, postpartum care guidance includes nutritional requirements during lactation, the importance of rest, and IFA supplementation. Finally, it details major Indian government schemes like Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY), Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram (JSSK), and Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana (PMMVY), which provide financial support and free services for institutional delivery and maternity care.

### **1. Birth Preparedness and Onset of Labor**
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] A birth preparedness plan involves active family participation throughout the pregnancy to ensure a safe and well-prepared delivery.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] A supportive companion should be chosen to provide emotional, physical, and practical support during labor and delivery.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] An essential delivery bag should be prepared in advance to avoid a last-minute rush.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] The delivery bag should include documents like Aadhaar card, maternal child protection card, all blood reports, and ultrasound reports.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] The delivery bag should also contain hygiene items (soap, towel, toothbrush) and clothes for the newborn and the mother.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] The healthcare center should be contacted immediately if labor begins or any complications arise, avoiding any delays in seeking medical care.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] Families should set aside sufficient funds for emergencies.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] Government ambulance services are available toll-free at 102/108.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] A family member or friend willing to donate blood in emergencies should be identified.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] For high-risk mothers, a referral or delivery plan at a Community Health Centre (CHC) or District Hospital (DH) should be arranged.
[Target:: BirthPreparedness | Milestone:: Pre-Labor | Action:: Guideline] Emotional support from the husband, mother-in-law, and other family members is crucial for the pregnant woman.

### **2. Immediate Newborn Care (The Golden Hour)**
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] Immediately after birth, the baby should be wiped dry and clean.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] The first bath for a newborn should be delayed for at least 24 hours.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] The baby should be kept warm with one or two layers of clothes more than adults and the head should be covered with a hat.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] The baby and mother should be kept together in the same room (rooming-in) to allow the baby to feed on demand.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] Skin-to-skin contact should be practiced as much as possible every day, especially when the baby is small.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends skin-to-skin care immediately after delivery for every newborn, irrespective of birth weight.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Guideline] Skin-to-skin contact calms and relaxes both the mother and the baby.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Benefit] Skin-to-skin contact regulates the baby’s temperature, heart rate, and breathing.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Benefit] Skin-to-skin contact stimulates digestion and an interest in feeding.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: ImmediateCare | Action:: Benefit] Skin-to-skin contact helps provide protection against infection.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Breastfeeding should be initiated within the first 60 minutes after birth.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Benefit] Early suckling helps make more milk, encourages the flow of breastmilk, and prevents early problems with breastfeeding.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Benefit] Early breastfeeding helps in the expulsion of the placenta and reduces bleeding in the mother.

### **3. Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC)**
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Definition] Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC) is a simple method of care for low birth weight infants that includes early and prolonged skin-to-skin contact with the mother (or a substitute caregiver) and exclusive and frequent breastfeeding.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Guideline] KMC is initiated in the hospital and continued at home until the infant needs it.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Benefit] KMC can prevent up to half of all deaths in infants weighing <2000g.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Benefit] KMC stabilizes body temperature, promotes breastfeeding, prevents infection and other morbidities.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Benefit] KMC leads to early discharge, better neurodevelopment, and encourages bonding between mother and infant.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Guideline] KMC is meant for stable Low Birth Weight (LBW) infants and denotes a sustained, long duration skin-to-skin contact.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Benefit] KMC is more effective than incubator care for stable newborns in providing thermal care, reducing nosocomial infections, improving exclusive breastfeeding and weight gain.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Guideline] KMC can be provided by mothers, fathers, and other adult family members who are willing, in good health, and free from serious illness.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Guideline] The KMC provider should maintain basic hygiene like hand washing, daily bath, clipped fingernails, tied up hair, and clean clothes.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: KMC | Action:: Guideline] Jewellery, watches, and sacred threads must be removed by the KMC provider as they may be a barrier to hygiene and might cause injury.

### **4. Initiating and Establishing Breastfeeding**
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Breastfeeding should be started within one hour of birth.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Benefit] The first milk, colostrum, is like the baby's first vaccination and protects the baby from illness.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Benefit] Colostrum acts as a purgative, helps clear meconium, and prevents jaundice.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Benefit] Colostrum is very rich in vitamin A, which is important for the eyes.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] The baby should be fed on demand, which is at least 8-12 times in 24 hours, day and night.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Early feeding cues include the baby bringing hands to mouth, turning head towards the breast, lip licking, and sucking movements. Crying is a late sign of hunger.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Let the baby feed for as long as they want on one breast to get both the foremilk (watery) and hindmilk (thicker, fat-containing).
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] After a cesarean section (LSCS), breastfeeding can be initiated in the recovery room, even while the mother is lying supine.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Breastfeeding positions include the cradle hold, cross-cradle hold, under-arm (football/rugby) hold, side-lying position, and reclining position.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Adequacy | Action:: Metric] Signs of adequate breastmilk supply include adequate weight gain (20–30 g per day in a full-term baby) and adequate urine output (more than six times in 24 hours).
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] For a good latch, the baby's mouth should be wide open, the lower lip turned out, and more areola should be visible above the baby's mouth than below.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] For good positioning, the baby's head and body should be in a line, held close to the mother's body, and their whole body should be supported.
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Initiation | Action:: Guideline] Signs of effective suckling include slow, deep suckles with pauses, audible swallowing, and the breast feeling softer after the feed.

### **5. Routine Newborn Care (First 28 Days)**
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] The birth of a child should be registered with the appropriate service.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] The umbilical cord should be kept dry, and nothing, such as ointment, should be applied to it.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] The baby should be tested for eye and hearing problems and for jaundice.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] Caregivers should wash hands with soap and water before handling the baby, before preparing food, and after using the toilet.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] The home and baby's clothing should be kept as clean as possible to protect from infection.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] For safe sleep, a baby should be placed on their back to sleep during the first year.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] A baby should sleep under a bednet in places where malaria is present.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Metric] Recommended sleep duration for infants up to 3 months is 14-17 hours per day.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] Infants should be physically active every day for at least 30 minutes.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone::RoutineCare | Action:: Guideline] Screen time should be avoided for children less than 2 years old.

[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] A health worker should be contacted as soon as possible if a child has any danger signs.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include the baby not feeding well or not eating or drinking enough.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include the baby having fast breathing.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include the baby having a high temperature, fever, or chills.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include the baby being less active than usual, having extreme weakness, or fainting.
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include severe abdominal pain or persistent backache (as observed by caregiver).
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include bleeding or fluid discharge from the vagina (in female infants).
[Target:: Newborn | Milestone:: HealthMonitoring | Action:: DangerSign] Danger signs include severe anaemia, indicated by a pale tongue and general body swelling.

### **7. Care for Low Birth Weight (LBW) Infants**
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Breast milk is the ideal food for all infants, including those who are Low Birth Weight (LBW).
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Many preterm infants have feeding difficulties due to inability to coordinate suck, swallow, and breathing, an immature gut, or systemic illness.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] LBW infants unable to take directly from the breast should be fed expressed breast milk by oro-gastric tube, feeding cup, paladai, or spoon.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Metric] Most LBW infants lose weight in the first few days, not exceeding 10-15% of birth weight.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Metric] LBW infants typically regain their birth weight by about 2 weeks.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Metric] After regaining birth weight, LBW infants should gain weight at a rate of 1.0% to 1.5% of body weight per day, which corresponds to 15-20 grams per kilogram of body weight per day.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] For infants below 1,500 grams, a postnatal growth chart should be used to plot weight daily until they are 40 weeks Post-Menstrual Age (PMA) or weigh 2500 grams.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Adequacy of nutrition for LBW infants can be monitored by frequency of urine (8-12 times per day) and weight gain (15-20 grams per day).
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] If an LBW infant has inadequate weight gain, the provider should check the amount of intake, assess attachment, and check for spillage.
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Complications like cold stress, sepsis, oral thrush, anaemia, and late metabolic acidosis can lead to suboptimal growth in LBW infants.

### **8. At-Birth Immunizations**
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Schedule] Under the Universal Immunization Programme, immunization starts as soon as the child is born.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Schedule] At birth, a newborn should receive one dose each of BCG, OPV (zero dose), and Hepatitis B vaccine.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Guideline] The Hepatitis B birth dose should be given within 24 hours of birth to protect from infection transferred from the mother. Maximum protection is provided if given within 12 hours.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Guideline] The OPV "zero dose" should be given within 15 days of birth.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Guideline] The BCG vaccine should be given as early as possible after birth and can be given up to one year of age.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Schedule] The BCG dose is 0.1 ml (0.05 ml for infants under one month) administered intradermally in the left upper arm.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Schedule] The Hepatitis B birth dose is 0.5 ml administered intramuscularly on the antero-lateral side of the mid-thigh.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: Schedule] The OPV-0 dose consists of 2 oral drops.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: SideEffect] After BCG administration, a scar forming is a normal reaction. It typically becomes red and hard after two weeks, may discharge pus, and leaves a small scar after 8 weeks.

### **9. Postpartum Care for the Mother (First 28+ Days)**
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] During lactation, additional calories and proteins are needed to maintain maternal health and support breast milk production.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Metric] During the first six months of lactation, an additional 600 calories of energy and 13.6g of protein are required daily.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Metric] From six to twelve months of lactation, an additional 520 calories of energy and 10.6g of protein are required daily.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Lactating women are advised to take daily supplements of iron and folic acid.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] The IFA supplementation regimen is 100 mg elemental iron + 500 mcg folic acid daily, continued for 100 days during the postpartum period.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Lactating mothers should consume plenty of fluids (over 2 liters per day).
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] A fiber-rich diet with whole grain cereals, pulses, and vegetables helps prevent constipation.
[Target:: PostpartumMother | Milestone:: PostnatalCare | Action:: Guideline] Putting the baby to the breast immediately after birth helps the uterus to contract firmly and reduces blood loss after delivery.

### **10. Relevant Government Schemes**
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSSK | Action:: Information] Under Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakram (JSSK), pregnant mothers receive free services at government health institutions, including medicines, diagnostic tests, nutritional meals, and transport.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSSK | Action:: Information] JSSK provides free delivery services (normal and caesarean).
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSSK | Action:: Information] For delivery, JSSK provides a free stay and meals for 3 days for a normal delivery and 7 days for a caesarean delivery.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSSK | Action:: Information] Under JSSK, sick children under 1 year of age receive free treatment, medicines, diagnostic tests, meals, and transport.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSY | Action:: Information] Under Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY), pregnant women from BPL families or Scheduled Castes/Tribes who deliver in a government hospital receive a cash benefit.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSY | Action:: Metric] In rural areas of Maharashtra, the JSY benefit is ₹700; in urban areas, it is ₹600, deposited into the woman's bank account.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: JSY | Action:: Information] The JSY benefit is provided for up to two live births and is transferred within 7 days of delivery.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: PMMVY | Action:: Information] Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana (PMMVY) provides cash compensation for the first living child to allow the mother to get adequate rest.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: PMMVY | Action:: Metric] Under PMMVY, ₹5000 is given for the first child in two installments: ₹3000 after early pregnancy registration and one ANC check-up, and ₹2000 after child's birth registration and completion of the first cycle of vaccinations (up to 14 weeks).
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: PMMVY | Action:: Metric] Under PMMVY, if the second child is a girl, ₹6000 is deposited in a single installment after birth registration and completion of the 14-week vaccination cycle.
[Target:: GovernmentScheme | Milestone:: PMMVY | Action:: Information] Eligibility for PMMVY includes women from families with income less than ₹8 lakh/year, SC/ST women, women with >40% disability, and several other categories.

## 4. Granular Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)
<secondary_faqs_block>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] Q: Can a mother with a fever, cold, or cough breastfeed her baby?
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] A: Yes. Common ailments like fever, cold, and cough are typically caused by viruses not transmitted through breast milk. The mother should wash her hands with soap and water and use a face mask to prevent spreading droplets. Continuing to breastfeed is safe and protective, as the mother's antibodies are passed to the baby through the milk.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] Q: Is it possible to breastfeed after a Cesarean section (LSCS)?
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] A: Yes. Breastfeeding can be initiated shortly after birth in the recovery room with assistance from a nurse. The mother can breastfeed in a lying-down position or, once able to sit up, use the football or cradle hold, taking care not to put pressure on the dressing site.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Adequacy | Action:: FAQ] Q: How can I be sure my baby is getting enough breast milk?
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Adequacy | Action:: FAQ] A: There are two reliable indicators: adequate weight gain (20–30 grams per day for a full-term baby) and sufficient urine output (passing urine more than six times in 24 hours).
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] Q: What should I do if I have pain around my nipples while breastfeeding?
[Target:: Breastfeeding | Milestone:: Challenge | Action:: FAQ] A: Nipple pain is often due to an improper latch, where the baby sucks only on the nipple instead of taking a mouthful of the breast. Ensure proper positioning so the baby's nose is against the nipple and they can take the nipple and the areola into their mouth. If nipples are cracked, you can express milk and feed with a cup and spoon while applying breastmilk to the nipples to help them heal.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: FAQ] Q: Is it normal for a scar to form after the BCG vaccine?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: FAQ] A: Yes, scarring is a normal reaction. About two weeks after administration, the injection site becomes red and hard, then forms a small elevation that may crack and discharge pus before healing into a small scar after about 8 weeks.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: FAQ] Q: Why is there pain and swelling at the injection site after vaccination?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: AtBirth | Action:: FAQ] A: For injectable vaccines like Hepatitis B, infants may have redness, mild pain, and swelling at the injection site. This is a normal reaction that typically resolves within 1-3 days. Cold compresses can be used to control swelling.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: FAQ] Q: How should a low birth weight (LBW) baby who is too weak to breastfeed be fed?
[Target:: LBWInfant | Milestone:: NeonatalCare | Action:: FAQ] A: LBW infants who are unable to feed directly from the breast should be fed expressed mother's milk using alternative methods like an oro-gastric tube, feeding cup, paladai, or spoon until they are strong enough to breastfeed directly.
</qa_pair>

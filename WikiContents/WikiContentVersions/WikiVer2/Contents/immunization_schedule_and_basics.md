## 2. Executive Guidance & Context Layer
<executive_brief_block>
### A. Long Comprehensive Summary
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] This document provides a comprehensive and verbatim extraction of the National Immunization Schedule (NIS) for infants, children, and pregnant women in India. It details the process of immunization, which protects a child against infectious diseases through the administration of vaccines. The schedule outlines which vaccines are given, at what specific ages, the precise dosage, the route of administration (e.g., oral, intramuscular, intradermal), and the diseases they prevent. For pregnant women, the schedule includes Tetanus-Diphtheria (T.D.) or Tetanus Toxoid (TT) injections to protect both mother and newborn from tetanus. For infants, vaccinations begin at birth with BCG, the birth dose of Hepatitis B, and the zero dose of OPV. The schedule continues with multiple doses of vaccines like Pentavalent, Rotavirus, IPV, and OPV at 6, 10, and 14 weeks. At 9-12 months, infants receive vaccines for Measles/MR and Japanese Encephalitis (in endemic areas), along with the first dose of Vitamin A. For children, the schedule includes booster doses of DPT and OPV, a second dose of Measles/MR, and subsequent doses of Vitamin A up to the age of 5. The schedule extends to adolescence with TT vaccines at 10 and 16 years. All immunization services under the NIS, including vaccines and supplies, are provided free of cost at government health facilities.

### D. Important Facts Summary
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] Immunization services under the National Immunization Programme are provided free of cost at government health facilities.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] For pregnant women, two doses of Tetanus-Diphtheria (T.D.) or Tetanus Toxoid (TT) are mandatory. The first is given when pregnancy is confirmed, and the second is 4-6 weeks later.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] A TT booster dose is given if the woman received two TT doses in a pregnancy within the last 3 years.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] At birth, infants should receive BCG (for Tuberculosis), OPV-0 (for Polio), and the Hepatitis B birth dose (within 24 hours).
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] At 6, 10, and 14 weeks, infants receive doses of Pentavalent, OPV, and Rotavirus vaccines.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] At 6 and 14 weeks, infants receive two fractional doses of IPV (Inactivated Polio Vaccine).
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] At 9-12 months, infants receive the first dose of Measles/MR vaccine and the first dose of Vitamin A solution.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] Booster doses for DPT and OPV are given at 16-24 months.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] A second DPT booster is given at 5-6 years of age.
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] TT vaccine is administered at 10 and 16 years of age.

### F. Top 5 to 10 Most Popular FAQs
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** What is immunization?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Immunization is the process whereby a child is protected against an infectious disease by administration of one or more doses of a vaccine, either by injection or by drops in the mouth.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** At what age should immunization start for any child?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Under the Universal Immunization Programme, immunization of children starts as soon as the child is born. Vaccination against childhood tuberculosis (BCG), polio (OPV) and maternally transmitted Hepatitis B (Hep B vaccine) are given to a child immediately after birth.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Why is timely vaccination important for children?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Vaccines ensure the best protection to children when they are given at the right ages, and in the required number of doses. At a certain period of life, there is a maximum incidence of a particular disease. Therefore, to prevent harm caused by the disease, vaccines are provided at that particular age. When children are not vaccinated, or if there is a delay in getting the vaccination, they remain unprotected and have increased chances of getting serious infection.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** How much does the vaccination cost?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** All immunization services including vaccines, syringes, Mother and Child Protection card and also medicines and supplements are given to all children free of cost, at the government health facilities.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** What vaccines are given to a pregnant woman?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Only Tetanus Toxoid (TT) vaccine is given to a pregnant woman. The source also mentions Tetanus-Diphtheria (T.D.) injections.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** If a child is sick, can she/he be vaccinated?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** A sick child suffering from mild illness (like cough, cold, or mild fever), mild diarrhoea or vomiting can be safely vaccinated with injectable or oral vaccines. However, a child who has some serious illness or is hospitalized (like in high grade fever, severe diarrhoea, etc.), should not be vaccinated until his or her condition improves.
</qa_pair>

## 4. Granular Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)
<secondary_faqs_block>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** What are the benefits of immunization?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Immunization protects children against certain specific and serious diseases, which otherwise would lead to stress, worry and expense of having a child become very ill and possibly die from a disease that could be prevented by administration of a vaccine.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** How do vaccines protect children from disease causing organisms?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Protection against different infections is due to the presence of some protective substances in the blood (known as “antibodies”), which kills the disease-causing organisms or neutralizes their effect when they enter the body. Vaccines contain a weakened form, killed form or part of the disease-causing agent (bacteria or virus), which when introduced into the child’s body by a correct route and technique activates a protective response against a targeted pathogen through generation of specific antibodies. These protective substances or antibodies further protect a child in case of a subsequent episode of infection.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Where should the parents take their children for vaccination?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** The parents/ caregivers can visit any government health facility, including hospitals, medical colleges, urban dispensaries, Primary Health Centres (PHCs), Community Health Centres (CHCs), sub-centres and Anganwadi centres to get their children vaccinated. In villages and some urban areas (like slums and mohallas), ANMs organize immunization sessions for providing immunization services to children.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Is scarring a normal reaction after BCG administration?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Yes. Scarring is a normal reaction after BCG administration. Two weeks after administration of BCG vaccine, the place of injection usually becomes red and hard. It then changes to an elevation in the skin which grows to maximum size by the fourth week. This later crack, discharges pus, gradually changes into crust (5-6 weeks), finally leaving a small scar (after 8 weeks).
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Why is there pain and swelling at the injection site?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** In case of injectable vaccines (like Hepatitis B, pentavalent, DPT and IPV), infants may have redness, mild pain, and swelling at the injection site. This is absolutely normal and goes within 1-3 days. This is not related to quality, safety or efficacy of the vaccines.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** What is the reason for fever coming after vaccination?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Occurrence of mild fever (specifically after Pentavalent and DPT vaccines) is the result of normal reaction that vaccines have on the body system. This fever is generally mild in nature, self-limiting, and goes within 1-2 days.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Why do pregnant women require vaccination against Tetanus?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Tetanus, also known as Lockjaw, is a life-threatening disease-causing seizures and severe muscle spasms, often leading to death by respiratory failure. In new-borns, disease presents as a tightly closed mouth due to spasm of jaw muscles. As a result, they cannot take feed, and in the majority of cases die. Tetanus Toxoid (TT) vaccine is given to all pregnant women to protect them and their new-born babies from Tetanus.
</qa_pair>

[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Question:** Is it safe to give TT doses in all pregnancies?
[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** Yes. Recommended doses of TT vaccine should be given to a woman every time she gets pregnant. Repeat doses only enhance immunity and do not cause any harm to the woman or her child.
</qa_pair>

[Target:: Immunization | Milestone:: Schedule | Action:: Extract] **Answer:** According to the National Immunization Schedule, one dose each of three vaccines, OPV, BCG and Hepatitis B, should be given to new-borns irrespective of the place of delivery. This is recommended for all institutional and non-institutional deliveries, in both public and private sectors.

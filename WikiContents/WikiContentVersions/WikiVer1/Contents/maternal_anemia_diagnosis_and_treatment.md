## 2. Executive Guidance & Context Layer
<executive_brief_block>
### A. Long Comprehensive Summary
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: overview | Scope:: summary] This module provides a comprehensive clinical framework for the diagnosis and management of maternal anemia. Diagnosis is based on hemoglobin (Hb) levels measured during antenatal check-ups, with anemia defined as an Hb level below 11 g/dl. The severity is categorized as moderate (Hb 7-11 g/dl) and severe (Hb < 7 g/dl). Treatment protocols are stratified by severity. Women with normal Hb levels (> 11 g/dl) receive prophylactic supplementation of one Iron and Folic Acid (IFA) tablet daily. Women diagnosed with anemia (Hb < 11 g/dl) are prescribed a therapeutic dose of two IFA tablets daily for at least 100 days. Monitoring involves a monthly Hb check to assess response. The protocol escalates for more severe cases: women with Hb levels between 7-8 g/dl may receive injectable parenteral iron, and those with severe anemia (Hb < 7 g/dl) require immediate referral to a higher facility (FRU/DH/MC) for intensive care, including continued parenteral iron therapy. In critical cases where Hb drops below 5 g/dl, hospitalization and blood transfusion may be necessary. Intravenous (IV) iron sucrose is an alternative for patients who cannot tolerate or do not respond to oral iron supplements.

### C. Contrastive Boundary Analysis (What This Document Is NOT)
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Context:: dosage_contrast] DOSAGE CONTRAST: Contrast the prophylactic dose against the therapeutic dose. For women with normal hemoglobin (>11 g/dl), the prophylactic dose is one IFA tablet daily. For women diagnosed with anemia (<11 g/dl), the therapeutic dose is two IFA tablets daily. Do NOT confuse these two regimens.
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Context:: intervention_contrast] INTERVENTION CONTRAST: Contrast oral supplementation against parenteral therapy. Oral IFA tablets are the standard first-line treatment for mild to moderate anemia. Parenteral iron (injectable) and IV iron sucrose are reserved for more severe cases (e.g., Hb 7-8 g/dl), non-responders to oral therapy, or those who cannot tolerate oral iron. Do NOT apply oral field protocols to severe cases requiring facility-based parenteral treatment.
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: care_level | Context:: location_contrast] CARE LEVEL CONTRAST: Contrast care levels based on severity. Mild anemia (Hb 9-11 g/dl) can be managed at the Sub-Centre level. Moderate to severe anemia (Hb <9 g/dl) requires referral to a higher-level facility like a PHC, CHC, or FRU for investigation and potentially more intensive treatment like parenteral iron or blood transfusions.

[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: diagnosis | Query:: screening_faq] Question: How is anemia diagnosed during pregnancy?
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: diagnosis | Query:: screening_faq] Answer: Anemia is diagnosed through a blood test that measures your hemoglobin (Hb) levels. <qa_pair>
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: monitoring_faq] Question: How often should my hemoglobin be checked after starting treatment for anemia?
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: monitoring_faq] Answer: After starting therapeutic IFA supplementation (2 tablets daily), your hemoglobin (Hb) level should be re-checked monthly to see if the treatment is working and if your levels are improving.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: severe_anemia | Query:: referral_faq] Question: At what point do I need to be referred to a bigger hospital for my anemia?
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: severe_anemia | Query:: referral_faq] Answer: You should be referred to a higher facility (like a PHC, CHC, or hospital) if your hemoglobin is below 9 g/dl, if it does not improve after a month of treatment with IFA tablets, or if you are diagnosed with severe anemia (Hb < 7 g/dl).
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: side_effects_faq] Question: What are the side effects of an iron sucrose injection?
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: side_effects_faq] Answer: Potential side effects of an intravenous iron sucrose injection include nausea, dizziness, low blood pressure, and in rare cases, allergic reactions. It is always administered under medical supervision.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: administration_faq] Question: Are there any foods or supplements I should avoid when taking my iron tablet?
[Target:: maternal_anemia | Milestone:: treatment | Query:: administration_faq] Answer: Yes. You should not take your iron (IFA) tablet at the same time as a calcium tablet, as calcium interferes with iron absorption. You should also avoid drinking tea or coffee with or soon after your meal or iron supplement, as they can make the iron unavailable to your body.
</qa_pair>

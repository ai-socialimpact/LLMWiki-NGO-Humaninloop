## 2. Executive Guidance & Context Layer
<executive_brief_block>
### A. Long Comprehensive Summary
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: schedule_overview | Action:: summary] This document provides a comprehensive and exhaustive reference for the National Immunization Schedule (NIS) in India, detailing the complete vaccination timeline for pregnant women, infants, and children up to the age of 16. For pregnant women, the schedule mandates Tetanus Toxoid (TT) or Tetanus-Diphtheria (Td) injections to protect both mother and newborn. For infants, vaccinations begin at birth with BCG, OPV-0, and Hepatitis B, followed by a series of multi-dose vaccines like Pentavalent, Rotavirus, IPV, and PCV at 6, 10, and 14 weeks. The schedule continues with Measles/MR and JE vaccines around 9-12 months, followed by booster doses for DPT, OPV, and second doses for Measles/MR and JE between 16-24 months. The schedule extends into later childhood with a DPT booster at 5-6 years and TT injections at 10 and 16 years. The document also includes critical FAQs addressing common post-vaccination reactions such as mild fever, pain, and swelling, and provides guidance on their management. It clearly defines contraindications, specifying that mild illnesses are not a reason to delay vaccination, whereas severe illness or hospitalization is. Furthermore, it outlines official catch-up immunization protocols for children who have missed doses, ensuring they can still achieve full protection.

### C. Contrastive Boundary Analysis (What This Document Is NOT)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: schedule_contrast | Context:: temporal_contrast] TEMPORAL CONTRAST: Contrast the routine immunization schedule against catch-up protocols. The routine schedule provides vaccines at specific ages for maximum efficacy (e.g., Pentavalent at 6, 10, 14 weeks). Do NOT confuse this with the catch-up schedule, which provides guidance for administering missed doses later, sometimes with different intervals or age limits (e.g., a child over 1 year who is unvaccinated receives DPT instead of Pentavalent).
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: reaction_contrast | Context:: severity_contrast] SEVERITY CONTRAST: Contrast mild, expected vaccine reactions against absolute contraindications. Mild reactions like localized pain, swelling, and low-grade fever are normal and can be managed with paracetamol and cold compresses. Do NOT confuse these with absolute contraindications like severe illness or hospitalization, which require postponement of vaccination until the child's condition improves.

### National Immunization Schedule: Pregnant Women
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: schedule] For pregnant women, the first dose of Tetanus Toxoid (TT-1) or Tetanus-Diphtheria (T.D.) is given early in pregnancy. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md, Ante natal care document.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: dosage] The dose for TT/T.D. vaccines is 0.5 ml, administered intramuscularly in the upper arm. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: schedule] The second dose (TT-2) is given four weeks after TT-1. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: schedule] If a woman received two TT doses during a pregnancy in the last three years, a single TT Booster dose is recommended. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md, Ante natal care document.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: timing] TT-2 or booster doses are ideally given before 36 weeks of pregnancy but can be administered later, even during labor if missed. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: safety] It is safe to give recommended doses of TT vaccine to a woman every time she gets pregnant; repeat doses enhance immunity and do not cause harm. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: pregnancy | Action:: side_effects] The injection site for T.D. vaccine may become slightly swollen, red, and painful, but these effects subside within a few days. (Source: Ante natal care document.md)

### Side Effects and Management
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: side_effects | Action:: bcg_reaction] A normal reaction to the BCG vaccine is a red, hard area at the injection site after two weeks, which evolves into a pustule, crusts over by 5-6 weeks, and leaves a small scar after 8 weeks. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: side_effects | Action:: injectable_reaction] Injectable vaccines like Hepatitis B, Pentavalent, DPT, and IPV may cause redness, mild pain, and swelling at the injection site, which is normal and resolves in 1-3 days. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: side_effects | Action:: management] For swelling and redness at an injection site, cold compresses can be used. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: side_effects | Action:: fever_reaction] Mild, self-limiting fever is a normal reaction, especially after Pentavalent and DPT vaccines, and typically resolves in 1-2 days. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: side_effects | Action:: management] Paracetamol (syrup or tablet, in divided doses) can be recommended for symptomatic relief of pain and fever in children after vaccination. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)

### Contraindications and Special Conditions
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: contraindications | Action:: mild_illness] A child with a mild illness like cough, cold, mild fever, or mild diarrhea can be safely vaccinated. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: contraindications | Action:: severe_illness] A child with a moderate to severe illness requiring hospitalization (e.g., high-grade fever, severe diarrhea) should not be vaccinated during the acute phase. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: contraindications | Action:: diarrhea_vomiting] Oral vaccines can be given to a child with mild diarrhea. For severe diarrhea requiring hospitalization, oral vaccines should be given after the diarrhea is controlled. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: contraindications | Action:: diarrhea_vomiting] Oral vaccines should not be given to a child who is vomiting recurrently until the vomiting is controlled. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)

[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: catch_up | Action:: rule] For an unvaccinated child over 16 months, the interval between Measles 1 and Measles 2 is 4 weeks, and for JE 1 and JE 2 (where applicable) the interval is 3 months. (Source: IMMUNISATION QUESTIONS FOR CHATBOT.md)

## 4. Granular Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)
<secondary_faqs_block>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: catch_up | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Question: If a child is brought late for a subsequent dose of multi-dose vaccines (for example, after more than the recommended one month), should the health worker restart the series from the first dose?
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: catch_up | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Answer: No. There is no need to restart the series or schedule again. If the child is brought late, you should give the next dose of the vaccine and motivate the parents to bring the child for the remaining doses at the recommended interval as per the immunization schedule.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: age_limits | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Question: What are the upper age limits for various vaccines?
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: age_limits | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Answer: According to UIP guidelines, the upper age limits are: BCG up to one year of age; OPV up to five years; Measles/MR up to five years; DPT up to 7 years; and JE up to 15 years. For pentavalent, IPV, PCV and Rotavirus vaccines, if at least one dose is given before one year, the schedule must be completed irrespective of age. If the first dose is not given before one year, these vaccines cannot be administered.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: infant_birth | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Question: Is there any time limit for these vaccines recommended for newborns?
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: infant_birth | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Answer: Yes. Hepatitis B (birth dose) should be given within 24 hours of birth (ideally within 12 hours). OPV (zero dose) should be given within 15 days of birth. BCG should be given as early as possible after birth, but not beyond one year of age.
</qa_pair>
<qa_pair>
[Target:: immunization | Milestone:: catch_up | Query:: healthcare_worker_faq] Question: If a child who has never been vaccinated is brought in at 9 completed months but before 12 completed months of age, then, can all the due vaccines be given to a child on the same day?
